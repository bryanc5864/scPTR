{\Large\bfseries scPTR: Decomposing Post-Transcriptional Regulation\\[2pt] at Single-Cell Resolution}
\vspace{4pt}
\end{center}

\section*{Introduction}

Single-cell RNA sequencing jointly captures spliced and unspliced transcripts, yet
computational frameworks have focused almost exclusively on transcriptional dynamics---most
notably RNA velocity, which models the rate of change of spliced mRNA abundance
[1,2]. Meanwhile, mRNA degradation---orchestrated by RNA-binding
proteins (RBPs), microRNAs, and \textit{cis}-regulatory elements---is a major determinant
of steady-state gene expression and is increasingly recognized as a driver of cell fate
transitions [3]. Despite this, no general framework exists to systematically
estimate per-cell mRNA degradation rates, discover cell populations defined by
post-transcriptional programs, or infer the regulatory networks governing mRNA stability at
single-cell resolution.
We present \textbf{scPTR} (Single-Cell Post-Transcriptional Regulatory Decomposition), a
framework that estimates per-cell, per-gene mRNA degradation rates ($\gamma$)
from standard scRNA-seq data and treats $\gamma$ as a primary analytical
axis---complementary to, and independent of, RNA velocity.

\section*{Methods}

For each gene, scPTR estimates the splicing rate $\beta$ as the 95th-percentile slope of
the unspliced/spliced phase portrait, then computes per-cell degradation rates via the
kinetic steady-state relation $\gamma_{ig} = \beta_g \cdot u_{ig} / s_{ig}$, where
$u$ and $s$ are $k$-NN Gaussian-kernel-smoothed counts.
PT states are discovered by PCA, $k$-NN graph construction,
and Leiden clustering in $\gamma$-space. PT velocity is
the weighted mean $\gamma$ difference between each cell and its neighbors,
yielding a vector field orthogonal to RNA velocity.
RBP--target networks are inferred via elastic net regression of target $\gamma$ on smoothed
RBP expression, with library-size partial correlation to correct for
destabilizing bias.
A structured VAE (DeepPTR) with a kinetic decoder disentangles
transcriptional ($z_T$) and post-transcriptional ($z_{PT}$) latent spaces under a negative
binomial observation model.

\section*{Results}

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,height=1.65in,keepaspectratio]{figures/fig2_validation.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Validation of $\gamma$ estimates.} \textbf{(A)}~Per-gene median $\gamma$
correlates with published mRNA half-lives (Spearman $\rho = -0.81$, sci-fate;
$-0.35$ to $-0.40$ in 10x data).
\textbf{(B)}~miRNA targets show elevated $\gamma$ vs.\ background
(126/215 families significant at FDR~$<$~0.05; aggregate $p = 4.7 \times 10^{-65}$).}
\label{fig:validation}
\end{figure}

Gamma estimates were validated against published mRNA half-lives across three
species--platform combinations, achieving Spearman correlations from $-0.35$ (10x mouse
developmental data) to $-0.81$ (sci-fate human metabolic labeling; Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}A).
Predicted miRNA targets from TargetScan~8.0 were enriched among high-$\gamma$ genes for 59\%
of 215 tested families (Fig.~\ref{fig:validation}B), and $\gamma$ correlated positively with
3$'$~UTR length ($\rho = 0.34$, $p < 10^{-200}$).

Clustering in $\gamma$-space revealed expression-invisible PT states in 3/8 pancreatic and
6/11 hippocampal cell types---subpopulations with distinct degradation programs yielding
higher silhouette scores in $\gamma$-space than expression space (Fig.~\ref{fig:findings}A).
These were enriched for coherent pathways: ER protein processing
in pancreas; synaptic plasticity in dentate gyrus.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\textwidth,height=1.65in,keepaspectratio]{figures/fig3_findings.pdf}
\caption{\textbf{Key findings.} \textbf{(A)}~Pancreas Epsilon cells form one
cluster in expression space (silhouette~$= -0.056$) but separate into distinct PT states in
$\gamma$-space (silhouette~$= 0.195$). \textbf{(B)}~Along pseudotime, $\gamma$
changes precede expression changes for 63--78\% of transition genes ($p < 10^{-5}$).}
\label{fig:findings}
\end{figure}

PT velocity was orthogonal to RNA velocity
(cosine similarity $\approx 0$) and degradation-rate changes
preceded expression changes for 63--78\% of transition genes
(Fig.~\ref{fig:findings}B). Library-size-corrected RBP--target network inference identified
hub regulators (HNRNPA1, YBX1, ELAVL1) validated by DepMap CRISPR essentiality
($p = 6.4 \times 10^{-5}$). Application to neuroblastoma revealed a predominantly stabilizing
RBP landscape (66\% stabilizing edges), contrasting with developmental tissues.

\section*{Conclusions}

scPTR reveals a previously inaccessible layer of post-transcriptional regulation
in single-cell data. By treating mRNA degradation rates as per-cell observables, scPTR
discovers expression-invisible cell states, shows that post-transcriptional rewiring
precedes transcriptional changes during fate transitions, and identifies essential
RBP regulators---establishing degradation-rate analysis as a complement to RNA velocity.
